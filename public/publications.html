Asymptotic Protection: Perfect Hedge Theory and Offshore Finance

Murad Farzulla · November 2025

Zenodo

Abstract: Complete hedging is impossible (Harrison-Kreps 1979 incomplete markets). Partial hedging extracts rents via necessity taxes, offshore variants enable regulatory arbitrage. Synthesizes theoretical impossibility, market microstructure, and offshore finance.

Version: v1.0.0 (completed November 23, 2025)

Core Thesis

Perfect hedging is mathematically impossible in incomplete markets (Harrison-Kreps 1979). Yet financial institutions sell "protection" at premium prices. This paper explores the gap between theoretical impossibility and marketed completeness.

Three-Part Framework

1. Theoretical Impossibility

  • Incomplete Markets: Not every contingent claim can be replicated
  • Basis Risk: Hedges correlate imperfectly with underlying exposures
  • Liquidity Risk: Market disruptions invalidate hedge assumptions
  • Counterparty Risk: Protection seller may default when needed most

2. Market Microstructure

  • Rent Extraction: Partial hedging sold as complete protection
  • Necessity Taxes: Systemic risk makes hedging mandatory (regulatory requirements)
  • Information Asymmetry: Sophisticated actors exploit less-informed counterparties
  • Concentrated Supply: Few providers → oligopoly pricing

3. Offshore Finance as Arbitrage

  • Regulatory Arbitrage: Offshore structures bypass domestic hedging requirements
  • Tax Optimization: Cross-border flows minimize effective tax rates
  • Opacity: Complex structures obscure ultimate beneficiaries
  • Consent Deficits: (Link to DoCS framework) - stakeholders excluded from governance

Empirical Agenda

Phase 2: Quantify rent extraction - CDS spreads vs actual default rates - Hedge effectiveness metrics across asset classes - Offshore premium vs onshore equivalent structures

Phase 3: Causal inference - Bloomberg Terminal analysis - Regulatory event studies - Network analysis of offshore ownership

Connections to Adversarial Systems

  • Finance as Adversarial Environment: Competing interests (hedger vs dealer, onshore vs offshore)
  • Friction Dynamics: Incomplete markets generate persistent misalignment
  • Stakes Heterogeneity: Systemic institutions vs retail investors

Doctrine of Consensual Sovereignty (DoCS): Operationalizing Political Legitimacy

Murad Farzulla · November 2025

Zenodo

Abstract: Stakes-weighted consent alignment framework operationalizing political legitimacy. 7 minimal axioms → 3 theorems (consent-holding necessity, inevitable friction, minimal absolutism). Monte Carlo validation across 4 dynamic mechanisms showing 98.5% friction reduction and monotonic convergence in 87.1% of runs.

Version: v1.0.1 (completed November 2025)

Core Framework

Legitimacy operationalized as:

L = w₁·α + w₂·P

Where: - α = stakes-weighted consent alignment - P = procedural fairness - F = friction (misalignment cost)

Minimal Axiomatic Foundation

7 Axioms: 1. Stakes heterogeneity (different impacts on different people) 2. Voice ≠ stakes (voting power may not reflect consequences) 3. Consent is measurable (preferences can be quantified) 4. Friction is costly (misalignment has real consequences) 5. Learning is possible (agents update beliefs) 6. No perfect information (uncertainty exists) 7. Stability preference (change has transition costs)

→ 3 Theorems: 1. Consent-holding necessity: Legitimacy requires stakeholder consent 2. Inevitable friction: Perfect alignment impossible under heterogeneity 3. Minimal absolutism: Unilateral authority limited to consent-preserving domains

Computational Validation

Monte Carlo simulations (1000 runs × 50 periods) across 4 mechanisms:

  • Stakes-weighted DoCS: α=0.872, F=1.6 (98.5% friction reduction)
  • Monotonic convergence: 87.1% of runs show consistent improvement
  • Bayesian learning: Agents update based on outcomes
  • Social learning: Information diffusion improves coordination

Applications

  • Algorithmic Governance: AI decision-making with stakeholder consent
  • Climate Negotiations: Emissions policy balancing development vs environment
  • Corporate Governance: Stakeholder capitalism frameworks
  • International Relations: Treaty design and compliance mechanisms

Trauma as Bad Training Data: A Computational Reframing of Developmental Psychology

Murad Farzulla · November 2025

Zenodo

Abstract: Trauma reframed as maladaptive patterns from suboptimal training data. Computational validation with PyTorch experiments demonstrating 1,247× gradient cascades, weight instability, overfitting (p=0.0012, d=3.08), and catastrophic forgetting. Nuclear families as limited datasets prone to overfitting parental dysfunction.

Version: v2.0.0 (completed November 22, 2025)

Core Thesis

Trauma is not "broken" psychology—it's maladaptive learning from bad training data. Just as ML models trained on corrupted datasets develop pathological patterns, humans exposed to adversarial environments during development encode suboptimal strategies.

Four Categories of Bad Training Data

  1. Direct Negative Examples: Extreme penalties in early training (abuse, violence, threats)
  2. Indirect Negative Examples: Noisy/contradictory signals (unpredictable caregivers, gaslighting)
  3. Absent Positive Examples: Class imbalance (neglect, emotional unavailability)
  4. Insufficient Exposure: Limited dataset size (isolated nuclear families)

Computational Validation

PyTorch experiments demonstrating:

  • 1,247× gradient cascade during adversarial training
  • Weight instability with high-magnitude parameter oscillations
  • Overfitting: Training loss 0.031 → test loss 2.847 (p=0.0012, Cohen's d=3.08)
  • Catastrophic forgetting: New learning destroys previous stable patterns

Nuclear Families as Limited Datasets

  • Small training corpus (2-4 primary examples)
  • No regularization (no external oversight)
  • Prone to overfitting parental dysfunction
  • Lack of diversity in relationship models

Implications

  • Clinical: Reframes therapy as model fine-tuning, not "fixing brokenness"
  • Policy: Advocates for expanded social infrastructure (childcare, community support)
  • Research: Novel interdisciplinary framework bridging ML, neuroscience, developmental psych

Cryptocurrency Market Event Study: Infrastructure vs Regulatory Shocks

Murad Farzulla · November 2025

SSRN / Zenodo

Abstract: Infrastructure events generate 5.7× larger volatility shocks than regulatory events in cryptocurrency markets (p=0.0008). TARCH-X and GJR-GARCH models with custom MLE estimation reveal asymmetric volatility persistence. GDELT sentiment decomposition and network analysis identify Ethereum (not Bitcoin) as systemic risk hub with 5× crisis amplification.

Version: v2.0.1 (published November 22, 2025)

Key Findings

  • Infrastructure > Regulatory: Technical failures (exchange hacks, network outages) cause 5.7× larger volatility responses than regulatory announcements
  • Ethereum as Risk Hub: Network analysis reveals ETH is the systemic risk connector, not BTC
  • Asymmetric Volatility: Negative shocks persist longer than positive shocks (leverage effect)
  • Sentiment Decomposition: GDELT Global Knowledge Graph sentiment analysis across 102 languages

Methodology

  • TARCH-X / GJR-GARCH with custom maximum likelihood estimation (standard libraries don't support GARCH-X)
  • Event Study Framework: 72 cryptocurrency-related events (2017-2023)
  • Network Analysis: Granger causality networks, systemic risk metrics
  • Sentiment Analysis: GDELT data with topic modeling and tone extraction
